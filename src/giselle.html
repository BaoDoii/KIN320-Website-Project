Film Review — Boxing — 1985

Rocky IV

Directed by Sylvester Stallone

Sport
Boxing
Director
Sylvester Stallone
Year
1985
Rating
★★★½☆

Giselle Gutierrez
Staff Critic — Reel Sport

While Rocky IV might be remembered best as the most on-the-nose political entry in the Rocky series, its impact is less about Cold War politics and more about how carefully crafted sports mythologies appear on screen. By staging boxing as both cultural performance and ideological conflict, Stallone crafts a very obvious narrative about sport, masculinity, and national identity. Rocky IV accomplishes this narrative by building systems of conflict through sporting technique, film style, and story.

Rocky IV is patriotic, but what makes it such an enduring sports film is the way it stages athleticism as mythologized performance that emerges directly from culture. Rocky isn't just fighting Ivan Drago in the ring, he's fighting culture itself.

Sporting Mythologies

Rocky IV establishes boxing as spectacle with national stakes from its opening minutes. Apollo Creed's introduction to his fight with Ivan Drago is extravagant. He wears bling, punches exuberantly, and walks out to loud music filled with confidence and swagger. Drago is introduced with no fanfare, expression, or personality. He stands rigidly and coldly as he enters the ring.

Apollo's style is stylized and emblematic of American sports entertainment culture, while Drago is stoic, a warrior constructed and conditioned by the Soviet machine.

Apollo dies during this fight, a decision that shocks Rocky and structures the conflict of the movie. From here on out, sport becomes deadly competition pitting the U.S. against the USSR.

Masculinity as Cultural Construction

Rocky IV is heavily invested in constructing Rocky Balboa and Ivan Drago as cultural symbols of masculinity. Rocky represents an older generation of masculinity forged through manual labor, masculinity cultivated through emotional connection with other men. His style of training is improvisational and gritty. Rocky pumps his own oil before heading to the gym and treats workouts as emotional performances.

Ivan Drago represents Soviet masculinity: orderly, stripped of visible emotion, and mechanized. His famous line during the build-up to the fight, "I must break you," dehumanizes his opponent and preaches cold war sports doctrine. Drago fights not for individual accomplishment but to best a technological version of America.

The film does construct Rocky through a nostalgic lens. Rocky trains with montages constructed with sweat, tears, creative workouts, and isolation. Drago lifts weights indoors and is surrounded by scientists and technology. Rocky embodies emotion; Drago becomes the embodiment of technological efficiency.

Rocky IV uses editing to create an illusion of equivalence between the two fighters. They go to similar lengths to train, hiring scientific experts and coaching staff. What makes Drago "different" in his training montage is the way the film frames him and constructs his labor as cold and isolated. Rocky's labor appears authentic because the viewer can see his sweat, loneliness, and perseverance while Drago's labor is broadcast.

The montage translates hours of labor into emotional performance storytelling. Sport becomes mythologized through movie editing.

Ideology in the Ring

Everything about Rocky's final fight with Drago is cinematic. There are nationalistic overtones to his victory with Russian crowd members eventually cheering for him throughout the fight. It is Rocky's emotion, and ability to grind through physical pain that carries him to victory. Stallone carefully crafts Drago and Rocky as dualistic warriors, but Rocky wins the fight through perseverance.

This commitment to fighting in the face of defeat is characterized as moral victory instead of strategic superiority. Rocky's ability to physically withstand Ivan's assault is framed as a testament to Rocky's character while Drago becomes the oppressor, choking Rocky in the ring after punching him senseless.

Rocky IV works so well as a sports movie because winning means more than victory. It stands in for an ideological triumph of the individual, Russian converts to cheering for Rocky's single fist carrying broad metaphors about the emotional truth of capitalism and patriarchy.

Technology vs. Masculinity in Sports Production

Rocky IV crafts conflicting ideologies of sports production through its characters. Drago represents the technological sport produced by communism. His training style is scientific and streamlined with data collected on him during his training. Rocky hires one trainer and mostly listens to his own thoughts, welling up with emotion at moments of personal reflection.

Rocky's brand of sports production is rugged, emotional, and stands in clear contrast to Drago's cold efficiency. Rocky is the self-made man in this story redeemed by love for his friend's widow and his ability to tap into emotional fortitude.

What Rocky IV ignores is that all sport at a high level is both technologically and chemically enhanced through obsessive training. No one is self-made. Even Rocky, the Product.

Conclusion

Rocky IV matters because it produces a very clear ideological reading of sport. The boxing match serves as political metaphor dressed up in spectacular filmmaking and overweight brunettes punching each other senseless.

Rocky IV tells us something about sports stories and how athletics get shaped into mass-consumable mythology. Sport is hardcoded with cultural meaning, whether we're watching Mockingjay: The Final Olympics or Daylight Savings boxing championships. Sports stories provide shape to how we understand what it means to be human.

Through Rocky Balboa, we imagine that individuals can fight against systems bigger than themselves and win by throwing punches.
